---
title: A Semester Reflecting on AI in My Work and Life
author: Maxime Argenson
order: 5
---

When I enrolled in this course, I expected my AI use to expand. Most of my peers seemed to be leaning into these tools more with each passing week: finding new prompts, new models, new shortcuts. Instead, something different happened to me, I used AI less. That shift turned out to be one of the most important things I learned this semester.

It started with the readings on AI and education. The research was striking in how clearly it documented something I had begun to sense in myself, that offloading cognitive work to an AI didn't just save time but eroded the mental habits that make thinking meaningful. Critical thinking, the ability to sit with a hard problem and work through it, is not a fixed trait but is a skill that shrinks when underused. The papers made that case with data, and I found myself unable to ignore it. I started asking a harder question: what am I getting out of this interaction, and what am I giving up?

That question reshaped how I approach AI tools entirely. I use them now with more deliberate restraint, reserving them for what I think of as "busy work" tasks, the kind that don't require original judgment, while pushing myself to engage directly with anything that requires genuine reasoning. Coding became my clearest test case for this. It would be trivially easy to paste an entire assignment into a prompt and copy whatever comes back. I have watched people do it. But when you skip the struggle, you skip the understanding. The errors you never had to debug, the logic you never had to trace, that gap accumulates, and eventually you find yourself dependent on a tool you don't truly understand. I made a conscious choice to use AI on a much smaller scale when coding: a confusing syntax question, a stuck point in a loop, a sanity check on an approach. Never the whole problem. I believe that constraint forced me to grow in ways that simply copying outputs wouldn't have.

Still, we live in a world where AI use is so common that opting out carries genuine costs.  Productivity gaps are real. Someone who uses AI well can accomplish in an afternoon what might take others a full day. Ignoring that entirely is not wisdom but it is stubbornness dressed up as principle. The goal seems to be finding a balance: using AI in ways that amplify your capabilities without substituting for them. That balance is personal, context-dependent, and harder to maintain than it sounds. But I think it is the right frame, and it is one this course helped me articulate.

The section on AI risks and mitigations also left a mark on me. What struck me most was the pace problem: AI capability is advancing faster than the regulatory and ethical frameworks designed to govern it. In most domains, we build guardrails before we open the highway. With AI, and especially with emerging technologies like autonomous agents, we seem to be doing it in reverse. The reading made clear that this is not a hypothetical concern. The potential for AI systems to operate outside meaningful human oversight is not a distant science fiction scenario.  It is a near-term engineering challenge, and the decisions being made (or not made) right now will shape what that future looks like. I came away from that unit more convinced than ever that regulation needs to lead capability, not trail it.

Amid those concerns, I found genuine excitement in the unit on AI in medicine.  AlphaFold stood out as one of the most remarkable things I encountered all semester. The idea that a system could predict the three-dimensional structure of a protein from its amino acid sequence, a problem that had stumped structural biologists for decades, and then make over 200 million of those predictions freely available to researchers around the world, felt like a genuine turning point. The downstream implications for drug discovery, agriculture, and material science are still unfolding. It was a reminder that AI, at its best, is not a threat to human ingenuity but is a multiplier of it. Similarly, the potential for AI-driven screening tools to reach patients in rural or low-income areas who lack access to physicians represents exactly the kind of asymmetric benefit that makes this technology worth taking seriously. These use cases don't get as much attention as the alarming ones, but they deserve to.

I am studying to work at the intersection of machine learning and healthcare, and the conventional pipeline runs through data analysis before reaching data science or ML engineering. The data analyst role, the entry point I am most qualified for right now, is also one of the roles most directly threatened by AI. Companies are not hiring junior analysts the way they once did, because AI can perform those tasks faster and cheaper. What that means, practically, is that the traditional apprenticeship model, where you build mastery by doing the unglamorous foundational work first, is disappearing from entire sectors of the industry. I am living the consequences of that shift as I enter the job market. There is something deeply ironic about studying AI in society while simultaneously being displaced by it.

The papers on AI companionship and mental health were perhaps the most surprising material of the semester. I had not appreciated how many people are using AI models as primary emotional outlets: sharing things with chatbots they would not say to a therapist or a friend. That phenomenon raises questions I do not have neat answers to. What I do know is that it has changed how I talk about AI to people outside the field. I used to describe it in terms of productivity. Now I am more likely to say this: AI is not a ground-truth machine, it is a prediction machine. It will give you confident, fluent, plausible-sounding answers that are sometimes wrong. Treat it the way you would treat advice from a very knowledgeable friend: worth hearing, worth thinking about, but not a substitute for your own judgment or for real human connection.

What ties all this together, looking back, is a shift in orientation. I came into this semester thinking about AI primarily as a tool, something to be used or not used depending on the task. I am leaving it thinking about AI as an environment, something that is actively reshaping the conditions under which people learn, work, create, and relate to one another. That shift in framing matters because it changes what questions you ask. Not just "how do I use this?" but "what kind of person am I becoming through how I use this?" I do not think that question has a permanent answer. But I think asking it, seriously and regularly, is where the real work begins.
