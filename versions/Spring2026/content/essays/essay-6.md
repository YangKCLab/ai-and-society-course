I originally enrolled in CS 481E/581E with a specific, technical goal: to gain knowledge in AI domains that would help my future research in programming languages. As a computer science student, I viewed AI primarily as a tool for optimization. However, as the semester continued, I realized that this course was not just about the "how" of AI, but the "why" and the "should or shouldn't." Over the semester, we have moved through a massive range of topics, and this journey has fundamentally altered my perspective. What I once saw as a purely technical field, I now see as a complex social force that requires as much ethical oversight as it does mathematical rigor.

My relationship with AI tools has undergone a significant transformation this semester. I use AI primarily as a sophisticated search engine, a way to find documentation or explain a specific error message or for getting summary of any research paper or documentation with me also reading the full paper and documentation.

The course topic that most profoundly changed my thinking was AI for information curation. Before this class, I thought of curation as a neutral process, simply a way to organize the vast amount of data on the internet. The shift occurred when we analyzed how these systems actually function as gatekeepers of reality.

I realized that the algorithms deciding what news we see or what research papers are "relevant" are not just helpful assistants, they are architects of our worldview. This mattered to me because it highlighted the invisible power of AI in the general labor market and online security. If an AI can curate information to the point of creating echo chambers, it poses a threat to democratic discourse. I was then thinking "how can we design languages that make the "thinking" process of these curation models more transparent to the end-user?" is this can be also possible.

A specific moment that surprised me occurred mid-semester while I was using a generative model to help refactor a complex piece of code. The AI suggested a solution that was incredibly elegant and used a library I wasn't familiar with. Positively, it saved me three hours of boilerplate writing. However, ten minutes later, I discovered a "silent" logical flaw that would have caused a memory leak in a production environment.

This was a surprise that taught me a vital lesson: AI has no "common sense" regarding the consequences of its output. It can be confidently wrong in a way that looks perfectly right. This experience was the most hands-on lesson regarding AI risks. It taught me that as we integrate AI into physical science and software engineering, humans must remain the final "logic gate."

I am hopeful about AI for science, specifically how it is accelerating scientific production in the social and physical sciences. The idea that AI can help us discover new materials or understand complex social dynamics faster is exciting.

However, I have deep concerns regarding the impact on the general labor market. We discussed how AI doesn't just automate tasks; it shifts the value of human expertise. For someone entering the field of computer science, I worry that the "entry-level" roles where we usually hone our craft are being swallowed by AI coding tools. I fear a future where the bridge between being a "student" and an "expert" is broken because the "middle-ground" work has been outsourced to a model.

Has my way of explaining AI changed? Absolutely. Before this class, I would explain AI to my family using metaphors of "brains" or "neurons." Now, I explain it as a "statistical mirror." I tell people outside the field that AI doesn't "know" anything; it simply reflects the patterns, biases, and data we have fed it over decades. When we discussed bias in AI systems, it became clear that the most important thing for a layperson to understand is that AI is not an objective truth-teller, it is a product of human history, complete with all our flaws.

Doing the course project was the most grounding part of the semester. While reading research papers gave me the "big picture," the hands-on project made me learn that the gap between a theoretical AI capability and a functional, reliable application is massive.

The limitations of current models in specialized domains. I realized that while AI is great at generalities, it struggles with the high-precision requirements of programming language semantics. This hands-on experience shaped my understanding that the "hype" around AI often ignores the grueling work of data cleaning, prompt engineering, and bias mitigation required to make a tool actually useful.

This semester was a constant encounter with ethical tensions. The most difficult for me was the discussion on AI companionship and AI-mediated communication. I felt a genuine tension between the benefits of these tools for those who are isolated and the potential for these tools to "de-skill" our ability to connect with other humans.

I navigated these dilemmas by adopting a "human-in-the-loop" philosophy. Whether it was discussing AI in healthcare and its adoption by doctors or the use of AI in education, I realized that the ethical "sweet spot" is usually where AI augments human agency rather than replacing it.

Finally, I am struck by how much more "whole" my understanding of AI has become. We covered everything: AI and mental health, governance and regulation, and even the learning experience and outcomes in our own classrooms. The daily updates and interesting articles we discussed in each class helped me see that AI is not a static field, it is a living, breathing conversation. Knowing everyone's thinking in our class discussions allowed me to see impacts I would have otherwise missed. I am grateful for the "harmful" and "helpful" perspectives shared this semester, as they have provided deep knowledge for future society development.
