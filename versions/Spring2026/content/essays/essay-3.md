If I had to pick one word for how AI felt this semester, it would be acceleration. Things that already seemed promising started feeling much more practical, and agentic building overall felt increasingly real over a relatively short period of time. I mean that both in the sense of building with these systems and building agentic systems themselves. At the same time, this course kept that from being just a technical observation. While I was seeing the acceleration directly from the engineering side, I was also being pushed to think more about the social, ethical, and relational implications of what exactly is accelerating and where it is starting to show up.

Over the semester, I scaled up my use of tools like Codex and Claude Code, and I also spent more time experimenting with continuous autonomous agent frameworks like OpenClaw, Hermes, and similar systems. What became increasingly interesting to me was not just using these tools as assistants in the ordinary sense, but thinking more in terms of orchestration, bounded execution, deterministic guardrails, and the surrounding system design needed to make them genuinely useful in practice. In other words, the interesting part was not simply that a model could generate something useful, but that these systems were becoming capable enough that the architecture around them mattered even more.

Over the semester I found myself thinking much more in terms of hybrid approaches to knowledge base construction, maintenance, and retrieval, especially in settings where the value is less in a single model call and more in how the broader system is composed. A lot of the real usefulness seems to emerge once the model is placed inside something more structured, where behavior is bounded, costs are controlled, and deterministic layers exist around it.

A specific moment that surprised me this semester was experimenting with whether Claude Code or Codex could one shot a full-stack web application from a single detailed prompt. I expected something at least somewhat useful, but I was still genuinely surprised by how far it got in one run. There were many gaps, but it functioned end to end in a way that was pretty striking. It is one thing to hear general claims that AI is improving rapidly, but another to watch a coherent application come together from one prompt in a way that would have seemed much less plausible not that long ago.

What I took from that was not that software engineering is somehow solved, but that the distance between idea and workable prototype has compressed a lot. There is still a major difference between something that merely works and something that is robust, maintainable, secure, and production-ready, and I do not think that distinction should be blurred. If anything, moments like that made the importance of engineering judgment even more obvious. Once the tools become highly capable, the remaining questions become more about reliability, maintainability, and trust.  At the same time, it is hard to deny how much leverage this already gives engineers.

One course topic that changed how I think about AI was AI companionship. That was one of the few topics this semester that felt somewhat outside my normal orientation, since I tend to think about AI most naturally in terms of systems, infrastructure, engineering workflows, and broader societal effects on labor or institutions. What stood out to me was that it made visible a dimension of AI use that is much more personal and relational. It was interesting to see how some users form bonds with these systems, sometimes casually and sometimes much more deeply, and that was not something I had really focused on before.

That topic mattered to me less because it changed my own direct use of AI and more because it expanded my sense of where AI is actually entering people's lives. It is easy, especially from a technical perspective, to frame AI mainly in terms of productivity, automation, and economic consequences. The companionship discussion pushed against that narrowness. It showed that AI is not just entering workplaces, software systems, or institutions, but also spaces of attachment and emotional reliance that are usually thought of as deeply human. Even though this does not affect me personally in that same way, it broadened my understanding of AI's societal role in a meaningful sense.

One of the biggest things I took from my project was a clearer sense of the current state of AI web scraping and browser interaction capabilities. These systems can do some genuinely impressive things in dynamic environments. They can write browser manipulation logic on the fly, interact with web pages adaptively, and in some cases use screenshots as part of how they navigate or decide what to do next. That kind of flexibility is difficult to ignore, especially compared with more rigid scrapers that can fail quickly once page structure changes.

At the same time, the project reinforced that these capabilities are strongest when paired with cheaper, deterministic, volume-based tools rather than treated as outright replacements for them.  The model driven approach is very strong where ambiguity is high, environments shift, or flexibility is needed, but if a stable deterministic scraper can do the job at scale more cheaply and more reliably, that's notable. So rather than coming away from the project thinking AI would replace conventional scraping outright, I came away seeing the hybrid structure more clearly. AI often seems strongest as a flexible layer around deterministic systems rather than as some universal substitute for them.

In terms of my daily work, my outlook on AI is primarily hopeful. These tools make it much easier for an engineer to move across languages, frameworks, and domains without paying the same friction cost that used to exist. They accelerate development, shorten iteration loops, and make it easier to ship useful things faster. The tools do not just save time in some vague sense. They materially expand what one person, or one small team, is able to build.

That hope feels concrete to me because it is grounded in direct use rather than hype. Over the semester I kept seeing examples where AI genuinely made building easier, faster, or broader in scope. Even when the outputs required review, the amount of initial lift the systems could provide was still substantial. That does not remove the need for expertise, but it does shift where that expertise is applied. More of the value starts to sit in orchestration, validation, architecture, and judgment, which is part of why I remain optimistic.

At the same time, one ethical and practical tension I kept thinking about this semester has to do with ownership and accountability. People often still want someone to truly own the work. They want a person who understands it, can stand behind it, and can be held responsible for it. As more labor becomes mediated by systems that are at least partially opaque, or partly externalized to model providers, that expectation becomes harder to satisfy in the conventional sense.

There is also a more literal business version of that same concern. If a company becomes heavily dependent on frontier model providers for critical workflows, then in some sense it may not fully own the labor pipeline it is relying on. If pricing changes, access changes, or an API disappears, the company could find itself in a precarious situation. That makes AI adoption not just a question of capability or return on investment, but also a question of control and resilience.

This semester also affected how I would explain AI to someone outside the field. I still think a longer discussion with analogies and foundational concepts is usually the best way to make the technology feel intuitive rather than mystical. But I think I would now place even more emphasis not just on how models work algorithmically, but on how they behave inside larger systems. That means explaining not only prediction, training, or prompting, but also retrieval, oversight, guardrails, dependency, and the distinction between bounded and unbounded execution.

Overall, the semester made AI feel further embedded for me. The acceleration in capability deepened my engagement with agentic building, both in the sense of building with these systems and building them directly. At the same time, the course broadened my thinking beyond engineering alone by pushing more reflection on social, ethical, and relational questions that are easy to underweight when looking only at capability. If there is one thing I take from the semester as a whole, it is that AI matters less as an isolated model capability and more as part of larger systems that are increasingly reshaping work, responsibility, control, and human life.
